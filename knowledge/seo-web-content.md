# SEO & Web Content

Standards for organic search and the web pages we ship. **SEO standards** (technical + on-page), **Helpful-content standards** (people-first content), **Landing-page standards** (structure and conversion), and **Screaming Frog crawl intake** (how a desktop site crawl is taken in and used). Apply these together when auditing, writing, or building web pages.


---

## SEO Standards

Baseline standards for how Saerens approaches organic search. Agents apply these unless a client's context gives a documented reason to deviate. These are agency conventions, not a substitute for current Google guidance — always defer to live search engine guidelines.

### Principles

- **Earn relevance, don't trick rankings.** Build pages people and search engines genuinely find useful; no manipulative tactics that risk penalties.
- **Match search intent first.** The page must answer the query type — informational, navigational, commercial, or transactional — before any optimization.
- **SEO complements paid.** Organic and Google Ads share keyword and intent insight; use SEO to own intent that paid is expensive for, and avoid cannibalizing.
- **Measure outcomes, not vanity.** Organic conversions and qualified traffic matter more than rankings alone (see `knowledge/measurement-reporting.md`).

### The three pillars

#### Technical SEO
- Site must be **crawlable and indexable** — check robots, sitemaps, canonical tags, and that important pages are actually indexed.
- **Core Web Vitals** and mobile experience matter — flag slow, unstable, or non-mobile-friendly pages.
- Use **structured data** (schema) where it fits — local business, products, reviews, FAQs.
- Avoid duplicate content, broken links, and orphan pages.

#### On-page SEO
- One clear **primary intent and target topic per page**; don't dilute across unrelated queries.
- Optimize **titles and meta descriptions** to be accurate, compelling, and within sensible length.
- Use a logical **heading structure** (one H1, meaningful H2s) and strong **internal linking** between related pages.
- Fill **content gaps** the client's audience is searching for; depth and clarity over keyword stuffing. Content must be genuinely helpful and people-first (see the *Helpful Content Standards* section).

#### Off-page / authority
- Build authority through **genuinely earned links and mentions**, not bought or spammy links.
- Prioritize **relevant, local, and reputable** sources for the client's market (Belgium/Flanders or the Netherlands).
- Be honest about authority: link building is slow; never promise volumes or timelines.

### Local SEO (important for lead-gen clients)

- Optimize the **Google Business Profile**: accurate categories, services, hours, photos, and reviews.
- Keep **NAP (name, address, phone)** consistent across the site and citations.
- Build **local landing pages** per service area where genuinely relevant — not thin doorway pages.

### E-commerce specifics

- Prioritize **category and product page** visibility and clean, crawlable faceted navigation.
- Avoid thin or duplicate product descriptions; ensure unique, useful content.

### Measurement

- Track **organic clicks, impressions, average position, and organic conversions** — tie SEO to business value, not rankings alone.
- Distinguish correlation from causation; ranking movements have many causes.

### Output structure (SEO Specialist)

Summary → keyword & intent map → technical findings → on-page recommendations → local SEO → off-page/authority → prioritized roadmap (impact/effort) → measurement → open questions/missing data → human approval required.

## Helpful Content Standards (people-first content)

How Saerens writes content for a client's own pages — landing pages, service and category pages, and supporting on-site content — so it is genuinely useful to people first. Grounded in Google's "Creating helpful, reliable, people-first content" guidance and the agency honesty rules. Pair this with the *SEO Standards* section (organic), the *Landing Page & Conversion Standards* section (conversion), and `knowledge/agency-foundations.md` (voice).

This is not in tension with conversion: a page that honestly answers the visitor's need converts better *and* ranks better. Writing for the reader and writing for results are the same job done well.

### Core principle

- **People-first, not search-engine-first.** Create content primarily to help a real, intended audience — never primarily to manipulate rankings or pad a page.
- The page exists to help a visitor reach their goal. If the main reason a page or paragraph exists is "to rank" or "to fill space", rethink it.

### The Who / How / Why test (apply to every page)

- **Who** — make it clear who stands behind the content: the real business, real proof, an honest about/contact. Trust comes from a visible, credible author/brand.
- **How** — be transparent about how content is produced. AI assistance is fine when it genuinely helps people, but a human reviews and owns every page; never publish unreviewed auto-generated filler.
- **Why** — the content is made to help the visitor, not to attract clicks for their own sake.

### People-first questions (aim for yes)

- Does the client have an existing or intended audience who would find this useful coming directly to them?
- Does the content show **first-hand experience and real depth** (actually installing the product, delivering the service, serving the area)?
- Does the page/site have a clear primary purpose?
- After reading, will the visitor have learned enough to act?

### Content & quality bar

- Original information, insight, or angle — not copied or thinly rewritten from competitors.
- Substantial and complete for the topic at hand; specific over generic.
- Descriptive, honest headings and titles — no clickbait, no exaggeration.
- Well produced: no sloppy spelling/style, no thin mass-produced pages.
- Worth bookmarking or sharing; more valuable than the other results a visitor could find.

### E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)

- **Trust is the most important.** Show it: real reviews, certifications the client can actually back (e.g. Official Google Partner), clear sourcing, accurate and current claims.
- Demonstrate experience and expertise relevant to the client's field and market (Belgium/Flanders or the Netherlands).
- Never invent facts, statistics, credentials, or testimonials — flag anything that needs client confirmation rather than fabricating it.

### Avoid (search-engine-first signals)

- Writing to a keyword or word-count target instead of to the reader; keyword stuffing or unnatural repetition.
- Mass-producing pages across many topics hoping some rank; thin doorway or near-duplicate location pages.
- Summarizing other sources without adding real value; fake freshness dates; promising an answer the page never delivers; leaving the reader needing to search again.

### How this applies at Saerens

- **Landing-page copy** must keep message match with the ad/keyword/query *and* genuinely answer the visitor's question — not just repeat a CTA.
- **Programmatic / local pages** need genuine information gain per page (ties to the *Helpful Content Standards* section), never thin duplication.
- All content is a **draft for human review**; nothing is published automatically.

## Landing Page & Conversion Standards

Baseline standards for how Saerens reviews and recommends landing pages so paid and organic traffic converts. Agents apply these unless a client's context gives a documented reason to deviate. The page is where spend turns into results — a strong page protects the whole investment.

### Principles

- **The page must match the traffic.** Message match between the ad/keyword/query and the page is the first thing to check — broken message match wastes spend.
- **One page, one job.** Each landing page should have a single primary goal and a single primary call to action.
- **Reduce friction, build trust.** Every unnecessary field, distraction, or unanswered objection costs conversions.
- **Helpful, people-first content.** The page must genuinely answer the visitor's question, not just push a CTA — see the *Helpful Content Standards* section. Helpful content converts better and protects organic visibility.
- **Recommend, don't deploy.** Agents produce specs, structure, and test hypotheses; a human/dev implements changes.

### Conversion essentials (review every page against these)

1. **Clarity above the fold** — within seconds the visitor knows what's offered, for whom, and what to do next.
2. **Value proposition** — clear, specific, and relevant to the traffic source; benefits over features.
3. **Single primary CTA** — prominent, repeated sensibly, with action-focused wording.
4. **Trust signals** — reviews, ratings, certifications (e.g. Google Partner), guarantees the client can actually back, real proof.
5. **Form / checkout friction** — ask only for what's needed; clear steps; visible progress for multi-step flows.
6. **Objection handling** — answer the obvious "but…" questions (price, risk, time, credibility).

### Recommended structure (adapt to goal)

Hero (value prop + primary CTA) → proof/trust → offer detail/benefits → objection handling → secondary proof → final CTA. Lead-gen pages center the **form/call**; e-commerce pages center the **product and add-to-cart/checkout** path.

### Technical & UX baseline

- **Mobile-first** — most paid social and much search traffic is mobile; the mobile experience is the primary experience.
- **Speed** — slow pages lose conversions; flag Core Web Vitals and heavy assets (coordinate with the *SEO Standards* section).
- **Accessibility basics** — readable contrast, sensible tap targets, labeled form fields.

### Measurement & testing

- Define the **conversion action** and confirm it's tracked (see `knowledge/measurement-reporting.md`) before drawing conclusions.
- Frame improvements as **A/B test hypotheses** with an expected direction; label hypotheses vs data-backed findings.
- Prioritize by **expected impact and effort**; never promise a specific uplift.

### Output structure (Landing Page / Web Design Specialist)

Summary → message match → conversion review → structure recommendation → copy & layout notes → technical/UX findings → prioritized recommendations (impact/effort) → test hypotheses → open questions/missing data → human approval required.

## Screaming Frog crawl intake (technical SEO)

How the brain receives technical-crawl data from Screaming Frog SEO Spider. Use
this when a workflow needs site-wide technical SEO health (broken links, missing
or duplicate titles/descriptions, missing H1s, indexability, redirect
chains/loops, slow or large pages).

### Why it works this way

Screaming Frog is a licensed **desktop** crawler, not a cloud API. The brain
runs in the cloud and cannot drive a desktop app on someone's machine. So crawl
data follows the agency's standard brain-vs-executor contract, in the
"semi-automatic" (Model B) shape:

1. The agency runs Screaming Frog on their own licensed machine (on a schedule,
   or manually) and exports a crawl.
2. A small push uploads that export to the brain.
3. The brain stores the **latest** crawl per client and reads it during runs.
   When no crawl exists yet, or it is stale, the deliverable says so plainly
   instead of inventing numbers.

The brain never starts a crawl itself and never claims a crawl is "live now"; it
reports on the most recent export it was given, with the crawl date attached.

### What to export from Screaming Frog

Export the **Internal: All** tab as CSV. That single file carries every field
the brain uses per URL: status code, indexability, title, meta description, H1,
response time, size, and redirect target. Column matching is tolerant, so minor
SeoSpider version differences are fine.

### How it reaches the brain

`POST /api/crawl-intake?clientId=<N>` with the CSV as the request body and the
header `x-trigger-secret: <SCREAMING_FROG_INTAKE_SECRET>` (falls back to
`AUTONOMOUS_TRIGGER_SECRET`). An optional `?crawledAt=<ISO>` records when the
crawl actually ran. The brain parses the export, derives Dutch signals, and
stores them on the client as `crawlLive` / `crawlLiveAt`, rendered into the
client doc under "Technical crawl (Screaming Frog)".

### How agents should use it

Treat the crawl block as read-only diagnostics. High-severity signals (5xx,
redirect loops, large numbers of 404s or missing titles) are real problems to
raise; info signals (non-indexable pages, large pages) may be intentional and
should be checked, not assumed broken. Always pair a recommendation with the
crawl date so the reader knows how fresh it is.
